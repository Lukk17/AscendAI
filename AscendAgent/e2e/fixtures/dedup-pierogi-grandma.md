## Origin

Grandma Maria Wiśniewska immigrated from Kraków to Chicago in 1962 and
brought her family's potato-and-cheese pierogi recipe with her. She made
them every Christmas Eve until her granddaughter inherited the recipe card
in 1998. The card lives in a wooden box on the kitchen shelf and is
transcribed below as accurately as the faded ink permits.

## Ingredients

- 500 g all-purpose wheat flour
- One whole large egg plus one extra yolk
- 200 ml warm whole milk (Maria always insisted on whole, never skim)
- A pinch of salt

For the potato-and-twaróg filling Maria used:

- 500 g floury potatoes (Yukon Gold or similar), peeled and cubed
- 250 g twaróg cheese (a Polish farmer's cheese; substitute well-drained
  ricotta or full-fat cottage cheese pressed dry)
- One medium yellow onion, finely diced
- Two tablespoons of butter
- A generous pinch of salt and several twists of black pepper
- A small pinch of dried marjoram (Maria's signature secret ingredient)

## Method

Maria mixed the dough in a stainless steel bowl. She combined the flour and
salt, made a well, and broke in the whole egg plus the extra yolk. She poured
the warm milk in slowly while stirring with a fork, then turned the
shaggy mass out onto her floured marble counter. She kneaded for about
**eight minutes** until the dough was smooth and elastic, then covered it
with a clean dish towel and **let it rest for forty-five minutes**. Maria
believed the longer rest produced a more tender result with the milk-based
dough; she would not be rushed.

While the dough rested Maria boiled the cubed potatoes in well-salted water
until they were soft enough to mash with a fork — about fifteen minutes.
She drained them thoroughly and let them steam in the colander for several
minutes to lose excess moisture. In a small skillet she sautéed the diced
onion in the butter over medium-low heat for ten or twelve minutes, until
the onion was deeply golden and sweet. She mashed the potatoes coarsely with
a fork (never a ricer — she liked some texture), folded in the twaróg, the
caramelised onion with all its butter, the salt, the pepper, and the
marjoram. She tasted and adjusted the seasoning, then let the filling cool
to room temperature before assembly.

To assemble the pierogi Maria rolled the rested dough out to about
two-and-a-half millimetres thick. She cut rounds with a sturdy water glass
about eight centimetres across. Each round received a generous teaspoon of
filling — Maria warned that under-filling produced disappointing pierogi.
She folded the dough over the filling, pinched the edges firmly with her
thumb and forefinger working from one corner to the other, and then crimped
them tightly with a fork. Maria laid the finished pierogi out on a
flour-dusted linen towel without letting them touch each other.

## Cooking

Maria cooked the pierogi in batches in a wide pot of salted boiling water.
She added them gently with a slotted spoon and stirred once with a wooden
spoon to keep them from sticking to the bottom. The pierogi rose to the
surface after two to three minutes; Maria let them cook for **three more
minutes after they floated** before scooping them out. She served them with
melted butter and a generous spoonful of sour cream on the side. Any
leftover pierogi were fried the next morning in bacon fat until crisp and
served with eggs for breakfast — Maria considered second-day fried pierogi
the only correct breakfast on the day after a Christmas Eve feast.
