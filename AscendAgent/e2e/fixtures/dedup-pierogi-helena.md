## Origin

Babcia Helena Kowalska learned to make pierogi from her grandmother in a small
village outside Lublin in the early 1950s. The dough recipe she taught her
granddaughters in the 1980s is the one transcribed below. Helena insisted that
the most important ingredients are time and patience, and that anyone who
shortcuts the dough resting step will produce pierogi that fall apart in the
boiling water.

## Ingredients

- 500 g all-purpose wheat flour, sifted
- 1 large egg, room temperature
- 250 ml lukewarm water
- 1 tablespoon sunflower oil
- A pinch of fine sea salt

For the cabbage and mushroom filling Helena used:

- 300 g sauerkraut, drained and chopped fine
- 50 g dried porcini mushrooms, soaked overnight in cold water
- One large yellow onion, diced
- 30 g unsalted butter
- Black pepper, freshly cracked, to taste
- A small handful of fresh parsley, chopped

## Method

Helena always mixed the dough in a wide ceramic bowl. She made a well in the
middle of the flour, cracked the egg into it, added the salt, and slowly
worked in the lukewarm water while stirring with a wooden spoon. Once the
mixture pulled together she added the tablespoon of oil and started kneading
on a floured wooden board. Helena kneaded for a full ten minutes by the
kitchen clock — never less. The dough is ready when it is smooth, elastic,
and slightly tacky but not sticky. She then formed it into a ball, covered
it with a damp linen tea towel, and **rested it on the counter for exactly
thirty minutes**. This rest is what lets the gluten relax so the dough rolls
out thin without snapping back.

For the filling Helena gently sweated the diced onion in the butter on a low
flame until it was soft and translucent but not browned. She drained the
soaked porcini mushrooms (reserving the soaking liquid for soup later that
week), squeezed them dry, chopped them coarsely, and added them to the onion.
After about five minutes she stirred in the chopped sauerkraut and let
everything simmer together for fifteen more minutes, seasoning with cracked
black pepper. The filling needs to cool completely before being spooned into
the dough rounds.

To shape the pierogi Helena rolled the rested dough out to about three
millimetres thick on her floured board. She cut rounds with a thin glass
turned upside down — about the diameter of a tea saucer — and placed roughly
a teaspoon of cooled filling in the centre of each round. She folded the
dough over to form a half-moon, pressed the edges firmly with her fingertips,
and then crimped them with the tines of a fork to ensure a strong seal.

## Cooking

The pierogi are cooked in a wide pot of generously salted boiling water. They
sink at first, then rise to the surface after about three minutes. Once they
are floating Helena let them cook for two more minutes, then lifted them out
with a slotted spoon. She served them immediately with melted butter and a
shower of crispy fried onions, or sometimes with a small dollop of sour
cream. Leftover pierogi were always pan-fried in butter the next day until
golden and crisp on both sides — Helena said the second-day fried pierogi
were even better than the first-day boiled ones.
